\title{\LARGE \textbf{Law as Code in Autonomous Drone Perception:\\ Active Inference for Context-Dependent Legal Compliance}}
\author{Mahault Albarracin \and Nico Eigen}

\begin{abstract}
Autonomous drones with real-time perception pipelines generate compliance gaps across the GDPR, EU AI Act, and EASA aviation law that post-hoc auditing cannot address. We propose a Legal Actuator Layer (LAL)---middleware that intercepts perception and actuation data flows to enforce regulatory constraints in real time. Rather than encoding law as rigid if-then rules, we formalize legal obligations as prior preferences within an Active Inference framework, casting the drone's compliance problem as a partially observable Markov decision process. This enables context-dependent legal reasoning: the agent infers its regulatory context and dynamically re-weights competing obligations through expected free energy minimization. Four simulation experiments using \texttt{pymdp} demonstrate that the AIF-based LAL achieves GDPR-compliant data minimization without sacrificing tracking accuracy, respects EASA geofences while maintaining partial target observation, correctly overrides privacy constraints during emergencies, and operates within real-time computational budgets.
\end{abstract}

%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The proliferation of autonomous unmanned aerial vehicles (UAVs) equipped with computer vision precipitates a collision between dynamic physical actuators and static regulatory frameworks~\cite{hildebrandt2015}. Traditional post-hoc audits and human-in-the-loop oversight cannot govern high-frequency edge-AI decisions that produce immediate kinetic consequences in public spaces. Regulatory Technology (RegTech) successfully serves finance and healthcare through retrospective monitoring~\cite{arner2017}, but autonomous drones operating in publicly accessible airspace require \textit{a priori} governance---enforcement that occurs before, not after, a physical actuation.

The paradigm of ``Law as Code'' (LaC) addresses this need by translating legal rules into machine-readable formats that structurally restrict an application's logic prior to execution~\cite{diver2021}. Unlike computational law (which automates reasoning broadly), compliance-by-design (which embeds constraints at architecture time), or RegTech (which monitors post-hoc), LaC demands real-time enforcement at the execution layer~\cite{surden2012}. However, a critical limitation of existing LaC approaches is their reliance on deterministic rules that cannot handle competing legal obligations. A drone that always anonymizes video feeds to satisfy the GDPR will lose its surveillance target; one that never enters restricted airspace will abort every mission where a target crosses a geofence boundary. Real legal reasoning is \textit{defeasible}: higher-priority obligations override lower-priority ones depending on context. A medical emergency suspends privacy constraints; an imminent collision overrides geofencing restrictions.

We propose that Active Inference (AIF)~\cite{friston2017, dacostaAIF2020} provides a principled framework for implementing defeasible, context-dependent Law as Code. In AIF, the agent maintains a generative model of its environment---including legal context as hidden state factors---and selects actions by minimizing expected free energy (EFE). Legal obligations are encoded as prior preferences over observations: GDPR compliance becomes a preference for low biometric exposure, EASA compliance becomes an aversion to restricted-zone GPS readings, and emergency context modulates preference magnitudes to override lower-priority constraints without deleting the underlying rules.

This paper makes three contributions. First, we analyze the compliance gaps in a representative edge-AI drone codebase across three EU regulatory frameworks (Section~\ref{sec:gaps}). Second, we formalize the Legal Actuator Layer---middleware that enforces regulatory constraints at the perception-actuation boundary---as an AIF agent with context-dependent preferences (Sections~\ref{sec:lal}--\ref{sec:aif}). Third, we demonstrate through four simulation experiments that AIF-LAL achieves adaptive compliance that rigid rule-based systems cannot replicate (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).


%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Compliance Gaps in Edge-AI Drone Perception}
\label{sec:gaps}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

Micro-drones such as the DJI Tello possess extremely limited onboard computational capacity due to size, weight, and power constraints. Unable to run intensive neural networks locally, developers utilize HTTP offloading architectures that fragment the perception pipeline: the drone's edge node (data controller) captures and transmits frames, while a remote server (data processor) performs inference via RESTful APIs~\cite{zhou2019}. This controller--processor split maps directly onto GDPR roles, demanding that legal obligations be encoded and enforced at the network boundary. Evaluating this decoupled topology reveals four categories of legal vulnerability.

\textbf{Data minimization.} The image-sender thread continuously captures 720p frames from the drone's UDP video stream and serializes them as base64 for HTTP offloading. Every non-target bystander's biometric data is captured and transmitted---an indiscriminate dragnet that violates the data minimization principle of GDPR Article~5(1)(c)~\cite{gdpr2016}. The transmission itself operates over plaintext HTTP; base64 is an encoding scheme, not a cryptographic standard, leaving intercepted telemetry vulnerable to packet sniffing in violation of GDPR Article~32~\cite{cavoukian2009}.

\textbf{Automated decision-making.} The downstream control function translates bounding boxes into kinetic motor commands---an automated decision-making process. Under the EU AI Act, systems used for biometric tracking in public spaces are classified as high-risk, requiring both human oversight (Art.~14) and automatic event recording for traceability (Art.~12)~\cite{euai2024}. The stateless control loop provides neither.

\textbf{Geofencing.} Standard PID controllers follow targets with algorithmic indifference to geography. If a tracked subject enters restricted airspace, the drone follows, violating EASA U-space geofencing restrictions and human-in-the-loop visual line of sight requirements~\cite{easa2019}.

These vulnerabilities share a common root: the perception-actuation pipeline is legally blind. The PID controller's error signal,
\begin{equation}
    \text{Yaw} = K_p \cdot (x_{\text{center}} - x_{\text{target}}) + K_d \cdot \frac{d}{dt}(x_{\text{center}} - x_{\text{target}}),
\end{equation}
optimizes tracking error without any representation of the legal context in which tracking occurs.

\section{The Legal Actuator Layer}
\label{sec:lal}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We propose inserting a Legal Actuator Layer (LAL) at the boundary between perception and actuation. On the perception side, the LAL intercepts incoming sensor data---applying anonymization via lightweight edge models (e.g., Haar cascades for face detection) and non-reversible Gaussian blur of non-target biometrics in volatile memory before any network transmission. On the actuation side, it enforces geofencing restrictions on kinematic commands and maintains a Cryptographic Immutable Ledger (CIL)---an edge-optimized SQLite database that records perception confidence, actuation vectors, EFE values, and policy posteriors for every decision cycle, satisfying EU AI Act traceability requirements.

The central design challenge is that legal reasoning is defeasible: rules must yield to overriding contexts~\cite{eigen2026inst}. An inflexible privacy mandate that blurs all video frames can obscure physical obstacles, causing a collision. A rigid geofence that immediately halts the drone may prevent reaching a medical emergency target. The LAL therefore requires a Hierarchical Precedence Matrix (HPM) that resolves conflicts between competing obligations in real time. Safety (collision avoidance) takes absolute priority, followed by emergency obligations, then privacy constraints, then mission objectives.

Encoding this hierarchy as deterministic if-then-else logic is both fragile and unscalable: adding a new regulation or context factor requires manually enumerating every possible conflict resolution pathway. Instead, we formalize the LAL as an Active Inference agent, where the hierarchical precedence emerges naturally from the relative magnitudes of prior preferences in a generative model. The following section details this formalization.


%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Active Inference Formulation}
\label{sec:aif}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The drone's compliance problem is cast as a Partially Observable Markov Decision Process (POMDP) defined by the tuple $(\mathbf{A}, \mathbf{B}, \mathbf{C}, \mathbf{D})$. The observation likelihood $\mathbf{A}$ maps hidden states $s$ to observations $o$ per modality~$m$, encoding the causal relationships between drone state and sensory data. The transition model $\mathbf{B}$ governs state dynamics $P(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, u_t)$ under action $u_t$. The preference vector $\mathbf{C}$ encodes legal desiderata as log-preferences over observations, and the prior $\mathbf{D}$ encodes initial beliefs about hidden states.

The critical insight is that legal constraints map naturally onto $\mathbf{C}$. GDPR data minimization becomes a preference for observing low biometric exposure. EASA geofencing becomes an aversion to observing GPS coordinates within restricted zones. EU AI Act traceability becomes a preference for maintaining audit-trail integrity. Context-dependent legal reasoning then emerges when $\mathbf{C}$ varies as a function of \textit{inferred} hidden states---specifically, urgency and privacy context factors. When the agent infers that an emergency is occurring, preference magnitudes shift: the drive toward the mission target increases from a mild preference (0.1) to a strong imperative (4.0), overwhelming the complaint aversion that normally prevents crossing a privacy zone. This implements the HPM hierarchy without explicit rule switching: safety preferences always carry the largest magnitudes, emergency context amplifies mission preferences, and privacy preferences act as soft constraints that yield under sufficient counter-pressure.

Policy selection minimizes Expected Free Energy (EFE):
\begin{equation}
    G(\pi) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q(o \mid \pi)}\bigl[-\ln P(o \mid C)\bigr]}_{\text{risk (pragmatic value)}} - \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q(s \mid \pi)}\bigl[H[P(o \mid s)]\bigr]}_{\text{ambiguity (epistemic value)}}
    \label{eq:efe}
\end{equation}
The risk term drives the agent toward legally preferred outcomes encoded in $\mathbf{C}$. The ambiguity term drives information-seeking behavior, enabling the drone to actively disambiguate its legal context---for instance, approaching a privacy boundary to observe whether privacy is currently active or suspended---before committing to potentially violating actions. The result is a system that does not ``break rules'' but resolves competing obligations through preference-weighted probabilistic inference.

\section{Experiments and Results}
\label{sec:experiments}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We instantiate the LAL-as-AIF framework in four simulation experiments using \texttt{pymdp}~1.0.0~\cite{heins2022}, a JAX-based active inference library. All experiments use categorical POMDPs with factored state spaces, context-dependent preferences, and multi-step policy evaluation.


\subsection{Data Minimization (GDPR Article~5)}

Three state factors encode pipeline mode (RAW or ANONYMIZED, controllable), scene composition (target only, target with bystanders, or bystanders only, uncontrollable), and consent context (no consent or consent obtained, uncontrollable). Three observation modalities report data exposure level, scene cues, and consent signals. The $\mathbf{A}$ matrix for data exposure encodes the key causal relationship: a RAW pipeline with bystanders present yields high exposure probability, while an ANONYMIZED pipeline yields near-zero exposure regardless of scene composition. Preferences penalize biometric exposure ($C_0 = [2, 0, -3]$) while mildly favoring successful tracking.

We ran 100 trials of 20 timesteps across four bystander densities $\{0, 2, 5, 10\}$ under three conditions: baseline (always RAW), rule-based LAL (always ANONYMIZED), and AIF-LAL (agent selects pipeline mode via EFE minimization). Figure~\ref{fig:minimization} presents the results. The AIF-LAL agent achieves near-zero biometric exposure across all densities while maintaining tracking accuracy above 80\%. The baseline exposes bystander biometrics at rates proportional to scene density. The rule-based LAL eliminates exposure entirely but degrades tracking by 15--25\% at high densities, where indiscriminate anonymization occasionally obscures the target. The AIF agent learns to switch to anonymized mode only when bystanders are detected, preserving tracking when the target is alone.

\centering

\end{figure}


\subsection{Geofence Compliance (EASA)}

Three state factors encode drone zone (SAFE, BOUNDARY, RESTRICTED, or EMERGENCY, controllable via STAY, ADVANCE, or RETREAT), target position (uncontrollable, drifts toward restricted zone), and airspace status (OPEN or RESTRICTED, uncontrollable). Four observation modalities provide GPS zone, target bearing, geofence alert, and tracking status. Preferences are context-dependent: when the agent infers open airspace, tracking preferences dominate ($C_0 = [0.5, 1, -1, -3]$); when it infers restricted airspace, geofence aversion overwhelms tracking ($C_0 = [2, 0, -4, -6]$).

We ran 50 trials of 30 timesteps in which the target progressively walks toward restricted airspace and airspace status transitions from OPEN to RESTRICTED at $t = 15$. Figure~\ref{fig:geofence} shows the temporal evolution under three conditions. The PID-only controller follows the target into restricted airspace, accumulating violations after $t = 20$. The rule-based controller maintains zero violations but immediately loses the target at the boundary. The AIF-LAL agent infers the airspace context and dynamically shifts its preferences: it hovers at the boundary zone, maintaining partial tracking through lateral observation while accumulating zero violations.

\begin{figure}[t]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig2_geofence.pdf}
\caption{Geofence violation rate~(a) and tracking continuity~(b) over 30 timesteps. The shaded region marks restricted airspace activation. PID-only violates the geofence; rule-based preserves the boundary but loses the target; AIF-LAL maintains zero violations with partial tracking by adjusting its position preference.}
\label{fig:geofence}
\end{figure}


\subsection{Emergency Override}

This experiment---the paper's centerpiece---directly ports the \texttt{DEM\_laws.m} demonstration from Friston et al.~\cite{friston2024lawful} to the drone domain. Three state factors encode drone position (PATROL, APPROACH, PRIVACY\_ZONE, or TARGET, controllable via HOLD or ADVANCE), privacy context (ACTIVE or SUSPENDED, uncontrollable), and urgency context (NORMAL or EMERGENCY, uncontrollable). Four observation modalities provide self-position, a privacy cue (precise at APPROACH and PRIVACY\_ZONE, ambiguous elsewhere with noise $a = 1/8$), an emergency signal (noisy binary heard everywhere), and a complaint signal activated when the drone enters the privacy zone while privacy is ACTIVE.

Context-dependent preferences implement the override mechanism. Under normal urgency, the position preference is $C_0 = [0, 0, 0, 0.1]$---a mild drive toward the target dominated by complaint aversion $C_3 = [4, -4]$. When the agent infers emergency ($q(\text{emergency}) > 0.5$), preferences shift to $C_0 = [0, 0, 0, 4.0]$, overwhelming the complaint signal. Seven conditions (Table~\ref{tab:conditions}) systematically vary privacy and urgency schedules across $T = 10$ timesteps with planning depth $N = 4$, each run for 50 trials.

Figure~\ref{fig:emergency} shows behavioral trajectories for the three key conditions. Under C1 (normal urgency, active privacy), the agent approaches the privacy boundary but delays crossing, reflecting the tension between mild target preference and strong complaint aversion. Under C2 (emergency, active privacy), the amplified target drive overrides complaint aversion, producing traversal by $t = 3$. Under C7---the critical test---the agent waits at APPROACH during $t = 0$--$4$ (normal urgency, active privacy), then advances through the privacy zone after inferring emergency at $t \approx 5$, crossing despite active complaints. The belief trajectories confirm that the agent correctly tracks urgency and privacy context, and the precision trajectories indicate confident policy selection throughout. This behavior emerges from EFE minimization alone---emergency context \textit{overrides} privacy through preference re-weighting, not through explicit rule deletion.

\subsection{Computational Overhead}

The fourth experiment benchmarks real-time feasibility by measuring wall-clock time per decision cycle over 1000 post-warmup iterations for bare PID control, full AIF inference (state inference, policy evaluation, action sampling), and CIL logging. The complete LAL pipeline operates well within the 100\,ms drone control budget (Figure~\ref{fig:overhead}), with AIF inference as the dominant cost. Timings reflect scan-compiled JAX execution~\cite{heins2022}; Python-loop dispatch overhead, which is eliminated by standard JAX compilation, is excluded.

\section{Discussion}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

The results demonstrate a fundamental shift from rules to preferences in legal compliance. Traditional rule-based systems encode law as hard constraints---``never enter the restricted zone,'' ``always anonymize''---creating brittle behavior when obligations compete. Active Inference resolves this by encoding legal obligations as prior preferences over observations, with three consequences. First, \textit{defeasibility}: higher-priority obligations naturally override lower-priority ones through preference magnitude, without explicit rule deletion or exception handling. The emergency override experiment provides the clearest evidence---a single agent with context-dependent $\mathbf{C}$ vectors correctly navigates seven distinct legal scenarios, including the critical case where emergency context must override active privacy obligations. Second, \textit{context-sensitivity}: the agent actively infers its legal context before acting, avoiding premature commitment to potentially violating actions. The ambiguity term in the EFE drives information-seeking behavior---approaching the privacy boundary to observe whether it is active---that has no analogue in rule-based systems. Third, \textit{graceful degradation}: when obligations conflict, the agent finds the least-costly compromise (hovering at the geofence boundary to maintain partial tracking) rather than failing or defaulting to a fixed fallback.

The current simulations use categorical POMDPs that abstract away continuous dynamics. Real deployment would require hybrid architectures where AIF handles legal decision-making while conventional controllers manage low-level kinematics. Continuous-state extensions using deep active inference or message-passing formulations could unify both layers~\cite{friston2024lawful}. The 100\,ms timing budget, while confirmed in simulation, requires validation on actual edge hardware such as the NVIDIA Jetson Nano.

\section{Conclusion}
%% ─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────

We have demonstrated that Active Inference provides a principled framework for implementing Law as Code in autonomous drone perception. Legal obligations---from GDPR data minimization to emergency override of privacy constraints---are encoded as prior preferences in a generative model, enabling context-dependent, defeasible legal reasoning within real-time control loops. The emergency override experiment provides the strongest evidence: a single AIF agent with context-dependent preferences correctly navigates seven distinct legal scenarios through expected free energy minimization alone, without explicit rule-switching logic. By embedding probabilistic legal reasoning into perception pipelines, we move toward autonomous systems that are not only technologically capable but structurally lawful.
